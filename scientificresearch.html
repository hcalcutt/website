Advancing Our Understanding of Star Formation

My research focuses on the complex physical and chemical processes involved in low-mass star formation, with particular emphasis on identifying evolutionary markers within Class 0 protostars.

Evolution Within Class 0 Protostars

Identifying chemical and physical markers of evolution in the earliest stage of protostellar development

Illustration of a Class 0 protostar showing key physical components including the envelope, outflows, and forming disk.

Project Overview

Classically the processes of low-mass star formation is subdivided into 6 key stages, where a single core evolves from a cold, dense prestellar core through to a hot core, with outflows and significant gas-phase chemistry and eventually to a planetary system. In reality, each star formation stage can be more complex. A large fraction of stars form in multiple systems significantly impacting how protostars evolve. Star formation processes can also be episodic, leading to significant changes in both the chemical and physical properties of sources.

A particular challenge for low-mass star formation studies is to disentangle effects due to evolutionary stage from other physical differences between different protostellar systems. The physical and chemical complexity observed in these objects and the surrounding star-forming environment, makes building a coherent picture of their evolution a challenge.

However, the Class 0 stage of low-mass star formation has a great potential to reveal specific tracers of evolution within an individual stage. Class 0 source are known for their rich chemistry, both within the warmer inner envelope and colder outer envelope, providing many potential chemical tracers of evolution. Additionally, many different physical attributes such as filamentary structures, outflows, and discs are detected at this stage. This research project seeks to determine the first clear markers of evolution within the Class 0 stage of low-mass star formation.

Research Methodology

This research employs a multi-faceted approach combining various observational techniques:

  • Spectral Line Analysis: High-resolution spectroscopic data to identify chemical signatures across different Class 0 sources
  • Interferometry: Detailed mapping of protostellar environments to trace physical structures at different scales
  • Radiative Transfer Modeling: Quantitative analysis of emission to determine physical conditions in different regions
  • Statistical Comparisons: Systematic comparison across a sample of Class 0 sources to identify evolutionary trends

Key Findings

Our research has revealed several potential evolutionary markers within the Class 0 stage:

  • Chemical complexity increases in a predictable sequence as Class 0 sources evolve
  • Outflow characteristics show systematic changes throughout the Class 0 phase
  • The ratio of specific complex organic molecules serves as a potential chemical clock
  • Disk formation processes can be traced through specific kinematic signatures

Protostellar Chemistry and Environmental Impacts

Investigating how different star-forming environments influence chemical complexity

Complex molecular structures within a star-forming region showing varying chemical compositions.

Project Overview

Star formation occurs in diverse molecular cloud environments, from isolated cores to clustered regions. This research program examines how these different environments impact the chemical evolution of protostars, with a focus on the development of complex organic molecules that may be precursors to prebiotic chemistry.

By comparing the chemical inventories of protostars in different star-forming regions, we can identify environmental factors that enhance or inhibit specific chemical pathways. This work has implications for understanding the chemical diversity of protoplanetary disks and, ultimately, planetary systems.

Research Methodology

Our approach combines observations with chemical modeling:

  • Survey Spectroscopy: Systematic spectral observations of multiple star-forming regions
  • Chemical Network Modeling: Detailed gas-grain chemical models under different physical conditions
  • Environmental Characterization: Mapping of radiation fields, density structures, and external influences
  • Laboratory Astrochemistry: Collaboration with laboratory teams to understand reaction pathways

Key Findings

Our research has revealed important environmental influences on protostellar chemistry:

  • Clustered star-forming environments show distinct chemical signatures compared to isolated cores
  • External radiation fields can significantly impact the development of complex organic molecules
  • Episodic accretion events leave lasting chemical signatures in the protostellar envelope
  • Gas-phase nitrogen chemistry is particularly sensitive to environmental conditions
